Article about Zebulon Pike

Introduction

Zebulon Montgomery Pike was an American brigadier general and explorer who became best known for the Rocky Mountain peak that now bears his name. As a U.S. Army officer he led two government expeditions through the recently acquired Louisiana Purchase territory. His first expedition (1805–1806) reconnoitred the upper reaches of the Mississippi River, while his second (1806–1807) ventured into what is now the American SouthwestL6-L13. Pike later published a widely read account of his journeys and rose to the rank of brigadier general. He served during the War of 1812 and was killed during the American attack on York (Toronto) in April 1813L64-L68.

Early Life & Education

Born on 5 January 1779 in Lamington, New Jersey, Pike was the son of Isabella Brown and Zebulon Pike Sr. His father had joined the Continental Army in 1775, and through him the family traced its lineage to Robert Pike, an outspoken critic of the Salem witchcraft trialsL71-L81. The younger Pike spent much of his youth at frontier outposts in present‑day Ohio and Illinois, then the northwestern boundary of the young United States. He received a commission as a second lieutenant of infantry in 1799 and was promoted to first lieutenant later that same yearL83-L87.

Marriage & Family

Pike married his cousin Clarissa Harlow Brown in 1801L88-L96. Of their five children, only their daughter Clarissa Brown Pike survived to adulthood; she later married John Cleves Symmes Harrison, son of future U.S. President William Henry HarrisonL88-L96. Pike was also the great‑granduncle of Medal of Honor recipient Emory Jenison PikeL88-L96.

Military Career

Early in his military career Pike served at various frontier posts, working in logistics and payroll. He came under the patronage of General James Wilkinson, who had been appointed governor of the Upper Louisiana Territory by President Thomas JeffersonL97-L107. In 1796 Pike accompanied French officer Georges Henri Victor Collot on a reconnaissance of the Mississippi valley, an assignment that gave him valuable experience in mapping and explorationL97-L107.

Exploratory Expeditions

First Expedition (1805 – 1806)

In the summer of 1805, General Wilkinson ordered Lieutenant Pike to locate the northern source of the Mississippi River, explore the northern Louisiana Territory and expel British and Canadian fur traders operating illegally in the region. Pike left St. Louis on 9 August 1805 in a pirogue. On 21 September he negotiated the Treaty of St. Peters with Dakota leaders at the confluence of the Mississippi and Minnesota Rivers, acquiring the future site of Fort SnellingL112-L124. After establishing a winter camp at the Swan River, the party continued upriver on foot, visiting British North West Company posts and informing traders that they were now subject to U.S. lawL124-L132. Pike persuaded Ojibwe chiefs to relinquish British medals and flags, hoisted the new fifteen‑star American flag and, during a side trip to Upper Red Cedar (Cass) Lake, designated that lake as the upper source of the MississippiL132-L145. He returned to St. Louis on 20 April 1806, completing the second U.S. government expedition into the newly acquired western territoriesL146-L152.

Second Expedition (1806 – 1807)

Upon returning from his first journey, Pike was ordered to lead a second expedition into the Southwest to explore the headwaters of the Arkansas and Red Rivers, survey natural resources and foster relations with Native communities. The broader purpose was to gather intelligence in case of a future American campaign against Spanish territories in New Mexico and TexasL159-L169. Beginning 15 July 1806, Pike’s party travelled west; in November they sighted a high mountain that he attempted to climb. Deep snow forced the men to turn back at Mount Rosa, but the peak, later named Pikes Peak, became a symbol of the expeditionL172-L175. Continuing southward, the group inadvertently crossed into Spanish territory and built a winter fort. On 26 February 1807 Spanish authorities arrested Pike and his men, escorting them first to Santa Fe and then to Chihuahua. While treated courteously, the men were held as prisoners and their papers were confiscatedL177-L190. Pike used the detention to study Spanish maps and learned of growing discontent in colonial MexicoL194-L200. Released at the Louisiana border on 1 July 1807, he returned to the United States with extensive knowledge of the SouthwestL200-L203.

War of 1812

Pike was promoted to captain during his second expedition and later fought at the Battle of Tippecanoe in 1811 under General and future president William Henry HarrisonL204-L210. He rose to colonel in July 1812 and served as deputy quartermaster‑general in New Orleans and inspector general during the latter stages of the War of 1812L204-L213. Promoted to brigadier general in March 1813, Pike commanded the advance guard of the American force that captured York (now Toronto) on 27 April 1813. As his troops approached Fort York, the departing British garrison detonated its powder magazine; Pike was mortally wounded by falling debris and died on the fieldL214-L226. His body was brought home and interred at the military cemetery in Sackets Harbor, New YorkL224-L226.

Confiscated Papers & Writings

During his captivity in New Spain, Spanish authorities seized Pike’s journals and maps. Although not recovered by the United States until the 20th century, Pike reconstructed much of his work from memory and published The Expeditions of Zebulon Montgomery Pike … in 1810L229-L239. The book became extremely popular—translated into Dutch, French and German—and provided American explorers with valuable information about trade routes and the future Santa Fe TrailL229-L240. Pike’s Spanish detention also gave him insight into political unrest in Chihuahua and the broader Spanish SouthwestL242-L245. A legend known as “The Lost City of Palanor,” which involves an alleged treasure and a hidden pre‑Columbian city, has been attributed to missing sections of his journalsL247-L253.

Legacy

In the years following his death, Pike’s military exploits were celebrated through biographies, memorials, paintings, poems and songsL256-L261. Numerous towns, counties and ships were named in his honour and natural features—including Pikes Peak, the second most‑visited mountain in the world—memorialize his exploratory achievementsL264-L266. His expedition route of roughly 3,664 miles is maintained today by the Pike National Trail AssociationL266-L267. Although his fame waned after the Civil War, it rebounded during the 1906 centennial of his Southwestern expedition. He is commemorated by a marble statue at the Antlers Hotel (erected 1901) and a bronze medallion in Monument Valley Park in Colorado Springs (1926)L269-L273. Historians continue to debate whether Pike was primarily an explorer or served covertly as a spyL272-L275.
